Terminator

Terminator is an old 2nd gen Intel i3 PC that I rebuilt with my brother. The laptop was completely damaged and broken, but the motherboard and display were still functional. The only issue was that it would shut down immediately after turning on because it couldn’t handle modern software and operating systems. After a lot of research and experimentation, we discovered that the processor was overheating due to an insufficient cooling system.

To solve this, we replaced the original cooler with a salvaged cooler from a damaged induction stove. To power this heavy-duty cooler, we cleverly repurposed the laptop’s 12V battery charging circuit. We removed the original battery because it had swollen into a "spicy pillow" and was no longer safe to use. The laptop’s speakers were water-damaged, so we replaced them with an old 12W audio system that was lying around. Again, we utilized the 12V supply to power the audio system. To house all the components, we built a custom wooden case designed for efficient airflow and organization. Despite its age, this PC now runs Windows 11 smoothly (after tweaking the OS to bypass TPM and security checks) and can even handle 4K video playback without any issues—all while keeping temperatures well below 70 degrees Celsius.

Pi based OctoPrint for 3D Printer

OctoPrint is an open-source web-based interface for controlling and monitoring 3D printers. It allows you to manage your 3D printer remotely through a browser, providing a wide range of features to streamline the printing process. OctoPrint is commonly run on a Raspberry Pi, which acts as a server to connect your 3D printer to your local network or the internet.

Raspberry Pi - NAS

I taught myself how to build a Raspberry Pi NAS using OpenMediaVault by exploring online guides, forums, and official documentation.
I started by researching the hardware requirements, choosing the right Raspberry Pi model, and gathering components like storage drives and a power supply. After setting up the hardware, I installed OpenMediaVault by following step-by-step tutorials and learned how to configure the system through its web interface. I experimented with setting up shared folders, user permissions, and network settings to make the NAS accessible on my home network. Along the way, I relied on online communities like Reddit and YouTube to troubleshoot issues and optimize performance. Now, I have a fully functional, locally hosted NAS that serves as a central hub for all my files, all thanks to self-guided learning and persistence.
